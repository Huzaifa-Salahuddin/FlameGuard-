Forest Fire Data Analysis

Visualizing Data for Better Insights
Boxplot of FWI

The boxplot of the Fire Weather Index (FWI) is crucial for visualizing the distribution and variability of fire danger levels across different conditions. By highlighting the range, median, and potential outliers, it helps researchers and decision-makers identify critical thresholds for fire risk, enabling better preparedness and response strategies to mitigate the impact of forest fires.

DC

The chart is a histogram showing the distribution of the variable "DC" for two categories: "Fire" and "Not Fire". The x-axis represents the values of "DC" and the y-axis represents the count of occurrences of each value. The green bars represent the count of "Fire" incidents, while the blue bars represent the count of "Not Fire" incidents. The chart shows that the majority of "DC" values for both categories are between 0 and 50, with fewer values occurring at higher ranges.

Box Plot of Whole Dataset

The boxplot also shows outliers, which are data points that are significantly different from the rest of the data. These are represented by individual points outside of the whiskers.

BUI

DMC

The histogram shows that the majority of "DMC" values for both categories are between 0 and 10. There are fewer occurrences of higher "DMC" values. The distribution of "DMC" values for "Fire" and "Not Fire" is similar, with both categories having a peak around 0-10. However, there is a slight skew towards higher "DMC" values for the "Fire" category compared to the "Not Fire" category.

FFMC

The histogram shows that the majority of "FFMC" values for both categories are between 60 and 90. There are fewer occurrences of lower "FFMC" values. The distribution of "FFMC" values for "Fire" and "Not Fire" is different. For the "Fire" category, there is a clear peak around 90, while for the "Not Fire" category, the distribution is more spread out with peaks around 60 and 70. This suggests that higher "FFMC" values are more associated with "Fire" incidents.

FWI

The histogram shows that the majority of "FWI" values for both categories are between 0 and 5. There are fewer occurrences of higher "FWI" values. The distribution of "FWI" values for "Fire" and "Not Fire" is different. For the "Fire" category, there is a clear peak around 0-5, while for the "Not Fire" category, the distribution is more spread out with a peak around 0-5 but with more occurrences of higher "FWI" values compared to the "Fire" category. This suggests that higher "FWI" values are more associated with "Fire" incidents, but there is still a significant overlap between the two categories.

The distribution of "ISI" values for "Fire" and "Not Fire" is different. RH

Temperature

Wind Speed vs Fire Spread

he histogram suggests a moderate positive relationship between wind speed and fire incidents in the region. While wind speed can contribute to fire spread, it is not as strong a predictor of fire occurrence as temperature.
